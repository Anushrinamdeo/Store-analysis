# Diagnosing a Retail Business: A Sales Intelligence Study

---

Most retail businesses don't fail because of bad products they fail because no one asks the right questions about the data sitting right in front of them. This project does exactly that.

Using transactional sales data from a U.S.-based retail company operating across Furniture, Office Supplies, and Technology, I conducted a full exploratory analysis to surface the decisions this business *should* be making but probably isn't.

---

## The Problem Worth Solving

Super Store sells to three customer segments Consumers, Corporates, and Home Offices across multiple U.S. regions. On the surface, the business looks healthy. Dig deeper, and a more complicated picture emerges: certain categories drag profitability down despite high sales volume, some regions are punching below their weight, and shipping preferences reveal something telling about customer expectations.

The core objective: identify where the business is leaking profit, where the real growth is hiding, and what a sharper strategy would look like.

---

## Questions That Actually Matter

Rather than a generic sales summary, this analysis was structured around business-critical questions:

- Which product category drives the most revenue and does that translate to profit?
- Are there sub-categories that look successful but quietly destroy margins?
- Which customer segment deserves the most strategic attention?
- Does the company's dominant shipping method align with profitability?
- Which regions and cities represent the strongest commercial opportunity?

---

## How the Analysis Was Built

**Tool:** Python end-to-end, covering data cleaning, transformation, visualization, and statistical analysis.

**Dataset:** Publicly available Super Store sales dataset via [Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com/akashkothare/tsf-datasets), containing order-level records with sales figures, profit margins, product categories, customer segments, shipping modes, and U.S. geographic data.

---

## What the Data Revealed

**Technology leads in profit, not just hype.** Despite Furniture generating comparable sales, Technology products deliver disproportionately higher margins a signal that category mix strategy deserves a serious rethink.

**Certain sub-categories are volume traps.** High sales rank doesn't equal high profitability. Some sub-categories actively suppress overall margins, making them costly to prioritize in marketing or inventory decisions.

**The Consumer segment is the commercial backbone.** It outperforms both Corporate and Home Office on profitability, which has direct implications for where acquisition spend and retention efforts should be concentrated.

**Standard Class shipping dominates and that's worth questioning.** It's the most-used shipping mode by a significant margin. Whether that reflects cost efficiency or customer dissatisfaction with alternatives is a question the business needs to answer.

**The West region leads in profitability. The Central region lags.** Geographic performance is uneven enough that a region-specific strategy rather than a blanket national approach would materially improve returns.

---

## Sharper Conclusions

This isn't a business with a sales problem it's a business with a *prioritization* problem. The revenue is there. The margin erosion is happening silently, through the wrong sub-categories, underserved high-value segments, and geographic blind spots.

A data-informed reallocation of resources toward high-margin sub-categories, the Consumer segment, and stronger regional focus could shift profitability without necessarily growing top-line revenue. That's a meaningful finding.
